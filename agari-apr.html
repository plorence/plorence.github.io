Agari Phishing Response

Agari Phishing Response is a tool designed to quickly triage employee-submitted phishing messages. I designed the product from its inception after on-site visits to several large Agari customers in the airline, publishing, and insurance industries. Regardless of their industry, email administrators at these organizations had the same fundamental problem: every employee in my organization has a "Report phishing" button in their email client; how can I quickly and efficiently respond to a constant stream of employee submissions of suspicious messages?

Through a series of interviews, we discovered that administrators followed the same general workflow:

  1. Begin the triage process.
    - Receive employee-submitted suspected phish message into a queue.
    Optionally contact the submitter and train on how to re-submit the message as an attachment to obtain the full, original headers instead of just forwarding the message.
    - Deconstruct the message into its component parts: message headers, attachments, URLs, message body content.
  2. Determine: Is this message bad?
    Using free or open-source tools, evaluate each component:
    • message header analysis (mxtoolbox.com or other DNS blacklist tools)
    • attachment analysis (VirusTotal or on-premises sandbox like FireEye)
    • URL analysis (VirusTotal or urlscan.io)
  3. Determine: What other similar messages came in? Who was impacted and how?
    Using internal search tools (O365 or Splunk), determine if other employees were impacted.
    Some users referred to this step as "determining the blast radius" of the malicious campaign.
  4. Take action: What should I do about it?
    - Remove messages from inboxes of all affected employees.
    - Document actions / results in a report or ticketing system.

We were aware of related tools in this space, but those tools were aimed more at employee training through phishing simulation. For example, a tool like KnowBe4 or Cofense/PhishMe can deliberately send well-crafted phishing messages to an employee base, track which users "took the bait" and therefore require additional training.

Most organizations also struggled with an initial triage, or scanning. For example, given a list of 45 submissions, where should we focus first on the newest submissions, or the oldest in the queue? It might make sense to focus on the oldest (a first-in / first-out strategy), unless, of course, those older messages proved to be benign.

Through interviews, we learned that administrators treated every user submission as a discrete "investigation," and their mental model mapped things like "assignee" and "remediation state" to each investigation. Some administrators reported spending 1-2 hours per investigation, and, in some organizations, they reported never being able to keep up with the constant onslaught of submissions.

We realized that in order to be valuable, the tool must at least provide an initial analysis (automation), and – like Agari Phishing Defense – having a human-readable rendering of the message contents would be extremely helpful in aiding quick confirmation, answering the question, "Is the employee reporting a true phishing attack, or is this just a benign (but annoying) marketing campaign?"

Because the tool is fundamentally a list management workflow (list/detail design pattern), the tool must provide easy sort and filter options on the index of investigations as well as a quick way to drill into the details of a selected investigation:

For each item (investigation) in the index view, we tried to fit as much succinct information into as small a space as possible. We employed progressive disclosure techniques to display additional information. Moving to a high-fidelity interactive prototype, we gathered more customer feedback from select users.

Users were generally happy with the approach and framing; their feedback was to get more succinct information and summaries into the investigations index.

Based on this feedback, we refined the "selected" state of an investigation on the index view to also show the HTML rendering of the message. For each user-submission, we created a "tile" to include the following pieces of information:

  1. Unique investigation ID
  2. Initial system-generated categorization
  3. Initial system-generated severity
  4. Assignee
  5. Reporter
  6. Timestamp of submission / age
  7. From and Subject of reported message
  8. Count of similar messages found (impact)
  9. Message Trust Score of reported message
  10. Count and analysis of URLs
  11. Count and analysis of attachments
  12. Count and analysis of domains
  13. Count and analysis of IPs

A tile could be selected to display the HTML rendering before drilling into the details around similar messages.

Agari Phishing Response employs several design tenets:

  1. "Follow the red." Given a long and complex list of components with varying indicators of "badness," a user only needs to focus on the red glyphs (like indicator lights in a dashboard) to determine which submissions require immediate attention.
  2. Every organization will have a different threshold and priority system. Strive to provide the necessary information consistently and objectively, and users will develop their own sense of how many indicators of "badness" equate to a critical/high/medium/low severity.
